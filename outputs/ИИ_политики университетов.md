# **Public University AI Policies in Russia: Sociotechnical Imaginaries at an Early Stage of Institutionalization**

**Abstract**

This article presents a systematic analysis of publicly available institutional documents regulating the use of artificial intelligence (AI) adopted by Russian universities at the early stage of institutionalization of this policy field. The corpus consists of ten documents — policies, regulations, rules, memoranda, and ethical declarations — adopted in 2024–2025. The study combines a structural-content analysis with an interpretive reading informed by the theory of sociotechnical and educational imaginaries. This two-level approach makes it possible to examine the documents not only as regulatory texts but as institutional articulations of desirable forms of human–technology relations in higher education. The analysis identifies a dominant regulatory imaginary within which three orientations co-exist: ethical minimalism, which frames AI primarily as a threat to academic integrity; a humanistic orientation, which positions AI as a tool subordinate to human cognition and autonomy; and a pragmatic-technocratic orientation, which treats AI as a resource of organisational efficiency. Agency is distributed asymmetrically across the corpus: students are constituted as the primary objects of regulation, teachers as intermediaries with local discretion, and administrators as supervisory actors, while education policy developers and decision-makers are rarely addressed. The regulatory provisions of the documents focus on risk management, disclosure, and academic misconduct; provisions that would address the transformation of teaching, learning design, or assessment are largely confined to declarative passages without implementation mechanisms. At this early stage, institutional effort is concentrated on normative control of AI use rather than on its pedagogical integration.

**Keywords:** AI in higher education; AI governance; university policies; sociotechnical imaginaries; educational imaginaries; document analysis; Russian higher education.

## **Introduction**

Artificial intelligence in education (AIED) is not a monolithic phenomenon. A recent scoping review identifies a wide range of practices under this umbrella: chatbots supporting teacher workflows, learning monitoring and action-logging systems, analytical tools for curriculum personalization, and more \[Tenisheva, Litvinova, 2025\]. This diversity of technical solutions is matched by a diversity of critical concerns: questions of academic integrity, assessment validity, model reliability, algorithmic bias, and discriminatory outputs have all emerged as pressing issues for researchers and practitioners \[Kuzminov et al., 2024; Jin et al., 2025\].

Managing this complexity has made AI governance in education an increasingly prominent field of inquiry \[Chan, 2023; Filgueiras, 2024; Ghimire & Edwards, 2024; Schiff, 2022; Wu et al., 2024\]. As Neil Selwyn notes, higher education is undergoing a transition from the logic of technology adoption to the logic of regulation and control, where questions of ethics, transparency, and institutional responsibility are moving to the centre \[Selwyn, 2019\]. In this context, universities face growing pressure to develop formal frameworks that set the terms for AI use across their communities.

This regulatory pressure is compounded by the speed of AI adoption among students \[UNESCO, 2023b\]. Institutional responses have ranged from outright prohibition to active integration \[Xiao et al., 2023\]. The University of Hong Kong, for instance, temporarily banned the use of ChatGPT in coursework \[Chan, 2023\], while the University of Sydney moved toward structured integration of AI tools \[Jin et al., 2025\]. A global UNESCO survey conducted in 2023 found that less than 10% of more than 450 educational institutions had developed formal AI guidelines — evidence of widespread fragmentation rather than coordinated policy \[UNESCO, 2023b\].

Russian universities have also begun to develop their own policies, regulations, memoranda, and ethical guidelines that establish principles and restrictions for the use of AI in educational, research, and administrative activities. This discourse has taken shape against the backdrop of a national strategy for AI development \[Presidential Decree No. 490, 2019\] and a national Code of Ethics for the Use of AI \[Artificial Intelligence Alliance, 2021\] — a context that makes the Russian case both distinctive and analytically interesting. However, this emerging body of institutional documents has not yet been systematically studied. Russian academic discourse lacks analyses of university AI documents in terms of their content and normative logic; there has been virtually no investigation of what educational values, assumptions, and visions of AI are inscribed in these texts; nor has there been analysis of how agency is distributed among the participants in the educational process: students, teachers, administrators, and those who develop regulatory decisions.

These gaps matter because institutional documents are not merely administrative instruments. Educational policy is also a site where collective ideas about technology, society, and the future are negotiated and stabilized. As Jasanoff and Kim show, policies and regulatory documents not only establish rules but express shared imaginaries \[Jasanoff, Kim, 2015\] — and in the field of education, as Rahm and Bouchard demonstrate, such imaginaries serve as tools for designing the “right” future and regulating the relationship between technology, pedagogy, and learners \[Rahm, 2023; Bouchard, 2017\]. University AI documents can therefore be read not only as regulatory acts but as elements of cultural construction — articulating what forms of human-AI interaction are considered acceptable, desirable, or ideal.

The present study proceeds from this premise. Its aim is to analyze what sociotechnical and educational imaginaries are inscribed in publicly available AI documents of Russian universities, and how these documents define the roles, responsibilities, and forms of agency of members of the academic community.

## **Sociotechnical and educational imaginaries**

University AI documents can be read in at least two ways. The first treats them as regulatory instruments: texts that define what is permitted, prohibited, and required. The second treats them as something more — as institutional articulations of a desired future, encoding assumptions about technology, human agency, and the proper organization of educational life. It is the second reading that this study pursues, and it is the concept of sociotechnical imaginaries that makes it possible.

Developed by Sheila Jasanoff and Sang-Hyun Kim, sociotechnical imaginaries are defined as collectively held, institutionally stabilized, and publicly enacted visions of a desirable future, grounded in shared understandings of what forms of social life and public order are achievable through science and technology \[Jasanoff, Kim, 2015, p. 19\]. The concept’s central claim is that technological trajectories are not determined by technical capacities alone — they are shaped by cultural values, political priorities, and institutional practices. Applied to university AI documents, this means that such texts do more than regulate behavior: they are one of the sites where shared visions of AI-mediated education are articulated, stabilized, and reproduced. Two clarifications follow for the scope of the present study. First, within this tradition policy texts are understood as performative — as instruments through which imaginaries acquire institutional durability — rather than as passive mirrors of pre-existing ideas. Second, our analysis addresses the imaginaries inscribed and stabilized in the documents themselves; it makes no claims about the effects of these documents on the behavior or beliefs of students and faculty, which would require different data and a different research design.

Lina Rahm extends this framework to the field of education, introducing the concept of educational imaginaries — a specific form of sociotechnical imaginaries in which the desired future is conceived as achievable through education \[Rahm, 2023, p. 51\]. This extension is analytically productive because it shifts attention from national-level technology governance to the institutional and pedagogical spaces where imaginaries are enacted and normalized. Drawing on Bouchard’s \[2017\] model of collective mythification, Rahm proposes an ideal-typical structure for analyzing educational imaginaries through four interrelated components: technologies, as objects of imagining and regulation; problematization, understood in the sense of Bacchi \[2009\] as the discursive process through which something is designated a “problem” requiring a specific “solution”; collective actors, the institutions and communities that shape regulatory expectations and claim authority to act; and target groups, those positioned as subjects and objects of educational transformation. Power relations are not a fifth element in this model but a relational property that permeates the interaction between all four components \[Rahm, 2023, p. 53\].

This four-component structure serves as the primary analytical instrument of the present study. It allows us to ask, systematically and comparably across the corpus, how AI is problematized in university documents, who is constructed as responsible for governing it, and what vision of the educational subject — student, teacher, administrator — is reproduced through these normative frameworks. The concept of problematization is particularly generative here: following Bacchi’s “What’s the Problem Represented to Be?” approach, we treat the “problems” named in AI documents not as pre-given realities but as discursive constructions that carry implicit assumptions about causality, responsibility, and legitimate response \[Bacchi, 2009\]. What a document frames as the problem of AI reveals as much about institutional values as about the technology itself.

## **AI governance in education**

University AI documents do not emerge in an institutional vacuum. They are shaped by a longer trajectory of algorithmic governance in higher education — one that predates the arrival of generative models and provides the conceptual infrastructure within which AI policy-making now operates. As Ben Williamson has shown across a series of studies, the turn toward datafication in education has progressively transformed learning processes, student behavior, and academic performance into data amenable to managerial intervention \[Williamson, 2017; 2018\]. Jeremy Knox develops this further, arguing that AI is embedded in broader “learning automation regimes” in which students are positioned as data sources and the pedagogical process becomes an object of digital control \[Knox, 2020\]. Generative AI does not break with this trajectory — it intensifies it, expanding the automation of tasks from content generation and personalized learning pathways to the identification of behavioral anomalies and suspected academic misconduct.

The Russian higher education system has followed a recognizable version of this trajectory. Over the past decade, federal digitalization programs have promoted the large-scale adoption of learning management systems, digital educational platforms, and unified information systems in universities; the COVID-19 pandemic accelerated the institutionalization of remote proctoring; and automated text-matching systems for detecting borrowings in student theses have long been a mandatory element of attestation procedures in most universities. The regulation of AI thus arrived in Russian universities not as the first encounter with algorithmic oversight of academic conduct, but as an extension of an already established infrastructure of digital control over the authenticity and accountability of student work. At the national level, this infrastructure has been framed by the state strategy for AI development \[Presidential Decree No. 490, 2019\] and by the cross-industry Code of Ethics for the Use of AI \[Artificial Intelligence Alliance, 2021\], which several university documents in our corpus explicitly invoke. Against this background, university AI documents are best understood not as responses to a sudden technological rupture, but as the latest institutional crystallization of a governance logic that has been building for years.

A body of empirical research has begun to document how universities in other systems are responding to generative AI. Wu et al. \[2024\], analyzing guidance documents from fourteen Big Ten universities, identify a predominantly advisory and role-differentiated approach, in which different institutional actors — IT departments, libraries, teaching centers — publish their own guidelines with little central coordination, and the primary burden of AI governance is delegated to faculty. Stracke et al. \[2025\], examining fifteen documents from eight European countries, find a similar fragmentation, with most policies focusing on teachers and students while administrators and policymakers remain largely absent as addressees. Both studies note the dominance of an academic integrity framing: AI is primarily constructed as a threat to authorship and assessment authenticity, with pedagogical transformation remaining peripheral. This is not incidental: as Perrotta and Selwyn \[2020\] argue, AI systems in education are not neutral instruments — they actively reinforce particular norms of conduct and reshape institutional expectations about transparency, efficiency, and manageability. Linderoth et al. \[2024\], approaching a comparable corpus through the lens of sociotechnical imaginaries, demonstrate that the ambivalence between AI as threat to integrity and AI as resource for innovation reflects not merely institutional caution but deeper disagreements about the purposes of education, the proper distribution of agency, and the normative horizons of policy.

These studies make a real contribution, but they share a significant limitation: they predominantly describe the content and distribution of policies without addressing the normative logic that underlies them. They document what universities prohibit, permit, and recommend, but do not systematically examine what visions of education, human-technology relations, and institutional order these documents reproduce and naturalize. This is precisely the gap the present study addresses. To move from descriptive mapping to interpretive analysis — to ask not only what AI documents say but what kind of educational future they inscribe — requires a different analytical register. It is to this register that we now turn.

## **Methodology and data**

Institutional documents are important material for understanding the organization of academic governance as a system of interconnected levels and practices \[Shankar et al., 2017\]. They function as critical structuring objects: they build relationships, distribute responsibilities and expectations, and shape local strategies and risk-assessment procedures \[Pawlicka-Deger, 2023\]. In the context of AI regulation, university documents act not only as normative texts but as the materialization of collective ideas about the proper interaction between humans, technology, and education.

The methodology integrates two analytical perspectives: a structural-content analysis of the documents and an interpretive reading through the lens of sociotechnical and educational imaginaries. The first level systematically describes the formal characteristics of the documents and makes them comparable with international cases; the second level reconstructs the meanings, values, and power relations inscribed in them.

**Corpus.** The empirical corpus comprises 10 documents of Russian universities regulating the use of AI in higher education, all adopted in 2024–2025 (Table 1). Data collection was carried out in \[MONTH–MONTH 2025 — to be specified\]. The corpus is not a sample drawn from a larger known population: it comprises all publicly available documents that met the inclusion criteria at the time of collection. The inclusion criteria were: (a) public availability on official university resources; (b) explicit designation as an official policy, regulation, rules, memorandum, or declaration approved by a university or its structural division; (c) substantive focus on the use of generative AI in educational, research, or related administrative activities. Documents were identified through targeted queries in search engines (Google, Yandex) using key phrases such as “policy on the use of artificial intelligence in education,” “regulations on the use of AI by students,” and “ethical principles of AI at the university,” complemented by manual searches of the official websites of \[N — to be specified\] leading Russian universities. The small size of the corpus reflects the early stage of institutionalization of this policy field: at the time of collection, formal public documents on AI had been adopted by only a small number of Russian universities. The study is deliberately restricted to public documents and does not cover internal regulations, local methodological materials, or informal practices.

**First-level analysis.** Each document was examined using a category-based comparative framework adapted from Stracke et al. \[2025\] and recorded in a common analytical matrix. For every case we coded: document type; scope of application; scale of action; level of the education system addressed (micro, meso, macro); policy focus (AI in education, AI literacy, or both); the mode of implementation (top-down or bottom-up); ethical grounds (meta-ethical, normative, applied) and ethical principles; and the relevance of the document to four target groups — students (TG1), teachers (TG2), education managers and administrators (TG3), and education policy developers and decision-makers (TG4). Relevance was assessed on a three-level scale: NR (the group is not mentioned and is assigned no normative role), SR (the group is mentioned or indirectly implicated, but without an elaborated set of responsibilities, rights, or expected actions — for example, when administrators appear only as recipients of reports), and R (the document explicitly defines the group’s responsibilities, expected conduct, authority, or role). Coding decisions were anchored in specific textual passages in which roles, norms, restrictions, and principles were explicitly formulated. Both authors independently read and coded all documents; divergent codings were resolved through discussion with return to the relevant passages, and recurring ambiguities led to refinement of category definitions. The results of the first-level coding are summarized in Table 1\.

**Second-level analysis.** The second level applied Rahm’s \[2023\] four-component model of educational imaginaries as a set of guiding analytical questions addressed to the patterns identified at the first level: how is AI problematized; what risks and opportunities are attached to it; which collective actors are positioned as responsible for its governance; and what vision of desirable educational order is constructed through these normative formulations. The components were operationalized through textual indicators. Problematization was traced through formulations framing AI as a risk, challenge, or object requiring intervention. The positioning of technology was traced through the role assigned to AI in academic work (tool, auxiliary instrument, driver of change). Collective actors were identified through indications of who formulates, interprets, and enforces rules. Target groups were analyzed through the degree and form of participation assigned to each group. A formulation was treated as a recurrent pattern when the same configuration was identified independently by both authors in at least \[two or more — to be confirmed against coding protocol\] documents in cross-case comparison; single occurrences were treated as case-specific variations. In addition, each substantive provision was classified as regulatory if it was accompanied by an implementation mechanism — a procedure, an assignment of responsibility, or a sanction — and as declarative if it articulated a value or aspiration without such mechanisms. This distinction is used throughout the second-level analysis and the discussion.

**Limitations.** Four limitations should be noted. The corpus covers only publicly available documents and cannot capture internal regulations or informal practices. The conclusions characterize the documents of ten universities and are not representative of the Russian higher education system as a whole. The study analyzes the imaginaries inscribed in the documents and not their reception or behavioral effects. Finally, the analysis of open textual sources cannot account for the strategic interests and contextual factors that shaped the drafting of each document.

**Use of AI tools.** In line with the journal’s ethical requirements, AI tools were used exclusively for auxiliary tasks. ChatGPT (OpenAI) was used to support bibliographic verification (identification of missing DOIs, publication metadata, consistency of references); DeepL was used for an initial draft translation of selected sections from Russian into English, subsequently revised by the authors. No AI tools were used for theory development, data analysis, coding, interpretation, or formulation of findings. The authors take full responsibility for the content of the article.

First, the corpus is structured by a duality of registers. Six documents are regulatory acts that prescribe procedures, assign responsibilities, and specify sanctions; four are declarative texts — memoranda and declarations — that articulate values and strategic orientations without implementation mechanisms. This duality is not a defect of the corpus but one of its central features: as the second-level analysis shows, the regulatory and declarative layers carry systematically different framings of AI.

Second, the distribution of agency across target groups is markedly asymmetric. Students are a relevant target group in all ten documents, and in the regulatory acts their obligations are specified in the greatest procedural detail. Teachers are addressed almost as widely, but in a different register: rather than being objects of detailed prescription, they are typically granted local discretion in setting the terms of AI use within courses. Administrators appear predominantly in supervisory and enabling functions, and education policy developers and decision-makers are explicitly addressed only in the two inter-university memoranda.

Third, virtually all documents combine, at the level of stated aims, a regulatory focus (AI in education) with a developmental one (AI literacy, critical thinking, responsible use). However, the two foci are realized in different registers: the regulatory focus is implemented through procedures and sanctions, while the developmental focus is articulated predominantly in declarative passages. This asymmetry between what is regulated and what is declared becomes the central object of the second-level analysis.

### **Second-level analysis: the imaginaries inscribed in the documents**

The second stage interprets these patterns through Rahm’s \[2023\] four components. For each component, we present the dominant pattern, the textual evidence on which it rests, and the principal variations across cases.

**First, at the level of technology,** AI is consistently described as a necessary and inevitable element of the academic ecosystem, requiring ethical regulation and rational inclusion in educational processes. In all documents, technology is positioned not as external or threatening, but as something to be “mastered” and integrated.

“…considers artificial intelligence technologies … an important tool that opens up broad opportunities for the development of education…” (Tomsk State University)

“…recognizes the importance of integrating innovative technologies, including generative artificial intelligence, into the educational process…” (RUDN University)

“…regards AI as a tool with the potential to enrich educational, research, expert-analytical, and administrative processes…” (HSE University)

However, in most documents this mastery is conceived in terms of control and subordination rather than co-creation: AI should be a tool, not an actor.

“…the responsibility for making informed decisions rests with the students and instructors, who use the technology as a tool rather than a replacement for human interaction and personal engagement or critical thinking…” (Skoltech)

“…AI is regarded as a tool that complements the work of the teacher, but does not replace or substitute for it…” (Tomsk State University)

“…AI / neural network tools, elements, and means serve as an auxiliary instrument for obtaining intermediate research results…” (GSM SPbU)

Only in a few texts — primarily the declarative documents — do elements of a developmental positioning appear, in which technology is seen as a source of rethinking pedagogical practices:

“…we support initiatives that strengthen the capacity of the Graduate School of Management, St Petersburg University, to understand the potential benefits and risks of generative AI for education and to develop new teaching methods, recognizing that generative AI tools are changing the ways educational content is created and delivered…” (GSM SPbU)

**Second, the problematization component** reveals that the dominant framing across the corpus constructs AI primarily as a threat to academic integrity rather than as a pedagogical question. In the regulatory acts, the “problem” of AI is not its implications for learning design or the teacher-student relationship — it is the possibility that students will use it to misrepresent their own work. The “solution” inscribed in response is correspondingly narrow: mandatory disclosure, verification procedures, and the threat of disciplinary sanction.

“There is no university-wide ban on the use of artificial intelligence by students — they may use generative model technologies… but in order to comply with academic norms they are obliged to indicate the results of their activities in which AI was used, specifying the nature and volume of work performed with its assistance. Otherwise, this is regarded as a violation of academic norms, which may entail disciplinary action.” (HSE University)

“Generative AI tools may be used by students in violation of ethics and conditions of assignments requiring independence, thereby undermining the value of learning assessment, certification, and qualification.” (RUDN University)

“The use of AI tools should be transparent, and a failure to acknowledge the use of AI will be deemed a breach of academic integrity…” (Skoltech)

In each case the discursive move is identical: AI enters the document as a potential threat, and the document responds with a compliance mechanism. Following Bacchi’s \[2009\] approach, the way a problem is framed determines which solutions become thinkable and which questions become invisible. When AI is problematized primarily as an integrity risk, questions about its pedagogical potential, its effects on assessment design, or its implications for student agency are not answered differently — they are not posed within the regulatory provisions at all.

Some documents gesture toward a broader problematization. The HSE Declaration states that “the goal of using AI technologies is not only to ease human labor, but to understand the influence of AI on the transformation of technological, socio-economic, political and cultural reality” (HSE University, Declaration). The GSM SPbU policy acknowledges that generative AI “is changing the ways educational content is created and delivered” and commits to “mastering new teaching methods.” These formulations constitute a qualitatively different problematization — AI not as a threat to be contained but as a force requiring new educational practices. By the criterion introduced in the methodology, however, these formulations are declarative: in neither document are they accompanied by procedures, responsibilities, or other implementation mechanisms. The conditions for a pedagogical problematization are present in the corpus, but at the time of analysis they had not been institutionalized in the regulatory layer.

**Third, the collective actors component** reveals two modes through which universities construct themselves as subjects of AI governance — and a significant absence. In the dominant mode, universities position themselves as ethical and normative centres responsible for establishing the terms of AI use within their institutional boundaries: the institution authors the rules, defines the principles, and sets up enforcement mechanisms. TSU’s policy is emblematic, designating a set of institutional bodies — an interdisciplinary research ethics committee, an Academic Council ethics commission, and an AI ethics ombudsman — as responsible for consolidating “intra-university mechanisms for addressing ethically contested situations” (Tomsk State University). Skoltech makes the norm-setting ambition explicit:

“Skoltech is the first university in Russia to adopt a local act specifically on the use of AI in education. Previously, our colleagues either introduced individual rules addressing particular issues or adopted documents exclusively concerned with laying out an ethical approach. We made an attempt to establish a legal framework.” (Skoltech)

A second, partially overlapping mode is the delegation of normative authority to individual faculty: the university sets the outer parameters, but teachers function as local interpreters and co-producers of the rules.

“The instructor may prohibit the use of generative models during assessment elements — in which case, they indicate this in the course syllabus. If such a prohibition is indicated in the syllabus, the instructor may award a grade of ‘0’ for work in which they detect signs of AI use.” (HSE University)

Skoltech similarly “reserves the right for instructors to define additional requirements for the use of artificial intelligence by students taking the course or doing a research project” (Skoltech). This construction positions the teacher not merely as an enforcer of institutional norms but as a governance actor whose professional judgment shapes the regulatory environment at the course level.

What is conspicuously absent from both modes is any construction of students as collective actors with a legitimate voice in shaping the rules that govern their own conduct. None of the documents articulates a mechanism for student participation in policy development. Students appear throughout the corpus as addressees of norms, not as participants in their formation.

**Fourth, the analysis of target groups** specifies how agency is distributed. Students emerge as the primary addressees of normative obligation: they must disclose, verify, declare, and demonstrate the authorship of their work, and the obligation is framed as a matter of moral responsibility. TSU’s policy links AI regulation to “promoting academic integrity and increasing the degree of moral responsibility of participants in the educational process” (Tomsk State University). RUDN operationalizes this through a dense sequence of personal obligations:

“The student must indicate in the abstract that generative AI was used in preparing the thesis… the student is obliged to provide detailed explanations and critical analysis of the results… the student is obliged to explicitly describe the methods and parameters of the AI algorithms used.” (RUDN University)

At the same time, the student is constructed as a potential violator whose conduct requires monitoring: HSE specifies that undeclared AI use in a final qualification thesis “may entail expulsion as a disciplinary measure” (HSE University). This double construction — the student as moral subject obligated to disclose, and as potential offender subject to sanction — is the defining feature of how target groups are imagined in the regulatory layer of the corpus. Teachers occupy a mediating and interpretive position, translating general norms into course-specific requirements; their discretionary authority, however, is oriented toward control — deciding what students may or may not do — rather than toward pedagogical experimentation. Administrators appear in supervisory and enabling capacities: ensuring that staff and students are informed, receiving reports, guaranteeing equal access to tools. Education policy developers and decision-makers are explicitly addressed only in the memoranda; in the regulatory acts the normative framework is presented as already given, its authors invisible.

### **Synthesis: three orientations within a regulatory imaginary**

The final step of the second-level analysis was a cross-case synthesis. For each document we juxtaposed three dimensions identified in the component analysis: the dominant problematization of AI, the positioning of technology, and the value vocabulary of the text. Recurring configurations of these dimensions across documents were grouped into orientations; a configuration was classified as dominant in a document when it organizes its regulatory provisions, and as present when it appears in declarative passages. This procedure yielded three orientations (Table 2).

The first — **ethical minimalism** — combines the problematization of AI as an integrity threat, the positioning of technology as an object of control, and a vocabulary of transparency, responsibility, and sanction. It dominates the regulatory acts. The second — a **humanistic orientation** — combines the problematization of AI as a challenge to human autonomy, the positioning of technology as a subordinate tool, and a vocabulary of human-centeredness, dignity, and critical thinking. It is most visible in the value declarations and preambles. The third — a **pragmatic-technocratic orientation** — combines the problematization of AI as an under-used resource, the positioning of technology as an instrument of efficiency, and a vocabulary of automation, optimization, and quality. It appears mainly in documents with an integrated or strategic scope.

Two features of this distribution deserve emphasis. First, the three orientations are not three separate imaginaries attached to different documents: most documents combine elements of at least two, and the corpus as a whole is organized by a division of registers — ethical minimalism dominates the regulatory layer, while humanistic and pragmatic orientations are concentrated in the declarative layer. In this sense, the three orientations are internal differentiations of a single overarching regulatory imaginary, in which AI figures as a managed object, the student as a regulated subject, and the university as a self-governing ethical centre. Second, what is missing from all three orientations is equally consistent: none of them articulates a vision of how AI might transform the educational relationship itself — teaching, learning design, assessment, or the formation of the educational subject. Where such a vision is gestured at, it remains declarative. We return to the interpretation of this absence in the discussion.

## **Discussion**

The findings can be summarized in three patterns: the dominance of integrity-focused problematization in the regulatory layer of the corpus; the asymmetric distribution of agency, with students as primary objects of regulation and policy-level actors invisible; and the concentration of developmental framings of AI in declarative passages without implementation mechanisms. In this section we interpret these patterns, relate them to the international scholarship, and specify the contribution of the Russian case.

Read against the trajectory of digitalization described above — the established infrastructure of text-matching systems, proctoring, and digital monitoring of student work in Russian universities — the dominance of the integrity framing appears not as a sudden reaction to generative AI but as the extension of a pre-existing governance logic into a new technological domain. The documents do not invent the figure of the student whose authenticity must be verified; they inherit it. This interpretation is consistent with Williamson’s \[2017; 2018\] and Knox’s \[2020\] accounts of datafication and learning automation, and it gives the regulatory imaginary identified in our analysis a specific institutional genealogy rather than treating it as a free-floating attitude toward technology.

The comparison with international cases can now be made precise. Our findings confirm two patterns documented in prior scholarship: the dominance of the academic integrity framing and the positioning of students as the primary addressees of regulation have been observed in American \[Wu et al., 2024\] and European \[Stracke et al., 2025\] universities alike. At the same time, the Russian case extends this scholarship in one significant respect. In the European corpus, fragmentation of approaches is plausibly attributable to differences between national systems \[Stracke et al., 2025\]; in the Russian corpus, comparable variation in document form, scope, and normative force (Table 1\) arises within a single national system that possesses both a state AI strategy and a national ethics code. The persistence of the same fragmented, integrity-centred pattern under these unified conditions suggests that this pattern is not primarily a product of inter-system diversity, but a structural feature of the early stage of AI normalization in higher education — a stage at which universities experiment with forms of regulation faster than any coordinating template consolidates. Whether this variation will subsequently converge is an empirical question that the present corpus, collected at a single point in time, cannot answer. Finally, our analysis modifies the picture offered by Linderoth et al. \[2024\]: where they locate the ambivalence between AI-as-threat and AI-as-resource in competition between different policy actors, in the Russian corpus this ambivalence runs through individual documents, distributed between their regulatory and declarative registers. Universities hold both framings simultaneously — but institutionalize only one of them.

This brings us to the finding that requires the most careful interpretation: the absence of a pedagogical imaginary. We are aware that a claim about absence demands caution, and two clarifications are in order. First, the claim is not that the documents say nothing about teaching: several texts mention new teaching methods, creativity, or the transformation of educational content. The claim is that, by the explicit criterion applied throughout the analysis, these formulations are declarative — none of them is accompanied by procedures, responsibilities, or mechanisms, in contrast to the integrity provisions, which are elaborated in procedural detail. The asymmetry between the two registers is itself the finding. Second, in the analytical tradition we follow, such a structured silence is not a gap in the data but a property of the problematization \[Bacchi, 2009\]: what a regulatory framework leaves without mechanisms is as informative as what it equips with them. Rahm \[2023\] describes this configuration as characteristic of regulatory imaginaries, in which technology figures as an object of management rather than a participant in educational transformation. In the Russian corpus, the pedagogical question is not answered negatively; it is deferred — held in the declarative register, available for future institutionalization but not yet part of the binding order.

The configuration of power relations reinforces this reading. As Jasanoff and Kim \[2015\] argue, sociotechnical imaginaries stabilize existing power arrangements by embedding them in technological frameworks. The hierarchy visible in the corpus — norms flowing downward from institutional authors, compliance flowing upward from students, the policymaking level structurally invisible — reproduces the traditional governance architecture of the university rather than transforming it. The delegation of interpretive authority to faculty in several documents constitutes an embryonic alternative, in which AI governance operates through professional judgment rather than central prescription; but this delegation remains confined to the dimension of control, and the absence of any mechanism for student participation in rule-making indicates how far the corpus stands from a dialogic model of AI governance.

The contribution of this study is therefore twofold. Empirically, it characterizes the founding documents of an emerging policy field at the moment of its formation — a moment that will not recur and that subsequent, more consolidated regulation will overwrite. Analytically, it demonstrates that the value of the educational imaginaries framework lies not in relabeling descriptive findings but in making visible the architecture of registers: the systematic division of labor between what universities regulate (integrity, disclosure, sanction) and what they declare (humanism, transformation, literacy). It is this division — rather than any single policy provision — that constitutes the dominant imaginary of AI in Russian higher education at the early stage of its institutionalization.

## **Conclusion**

This study set out to analyze what sociotechnical and educational imaginaries are inscribed in the publicly available AI documents of Russian universities. The analysis of ten documents adopted in 2024–2025 — the founding corpus of this policy field in Russia — shows that universities are actively producing institutional responses to generative AI, but that these responses are concentrated in a compliance register: defining what is permitted, prohibited, and declared, rather than articulating what AI means for the purposes and practices of education. The dominant imaginary is one of normative control, internally differentiated into three orientations — ethical minimalism, humanistic, and pragmatic-technocratic — whose distribution across the regulatory and declarative registers of the documents is itself the central structural finding.

The Russian case contributes to the international scholarship in two ways. First, it shows that the integrity-focused, student-centred pattern of regulation documented in European and American contexts persists in a nationally unified institutional environment, which supports interpreting this pattern as a structural feature of early AI normalization rather than a product of system diversity. Second, it demonstrates the analytical value of the educational imaginaries framework for document analysis: the framework makes visible not only what policies say but how they distribute their claims between binding and declarative registers — and what they thereby defer.

Future work should follow this field as it consolidates: whether the declarative layer of the current documents — the humanistic and developmental commitments without mechanisms — becomes institutionalized, and whether the embryonic alternatives identified here, faculty discretion and inter-university coordination, develop into dominant patterns. Equally important is research that moves beyond documents to reception: how the imaginaries inscribed in these texts are enacted, contested, or ignored in the everyday practice of teaching and learning. The question that the current corpus leaves open is, in the end, a normative one: under what institutional conditions universities will move from managing AI as a risk to engaging it as a resource for rethinking what education is for.
